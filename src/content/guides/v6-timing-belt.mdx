The 6G72 (3.0L) and 6G74 (3.5L) V6 petrol engines use a timing belt driving dual overhead camshafts. Belt failure will cause catastrophic valve/piston damage as these are interference engines — a snapped belt at any speed will bend valves. Replacement interval is 90,000–100,000 km or 5 years (whichever comes first). Always replace the water pump, belt tensioner, and idler at the same time as they share the same labour. This is an expert-level job requiring engine disassembly and careful timing mark alignment.

## Symptoms

- Cracking or glazing visible on belt surface (inspect through inspection cover)
- Engine suddenly dies and will not restart (belt snapped)
- Rough running, backfiring, or poor power after extended high-mileage service
- Squealing from the front of the engine (worn tensioner bearing)

## Tools needed

- Full socket set (metric)
- Torque wrench (up to 200 Nm for crankshaft pulley bolt)
- Crankshaft pulley holding tool or strap wrench
- Timing belt tension gauge (preferred) or factory tensioner tool
- Coolant drain tray and hose
- Marker or whiteout pen (for marking TDC and timing reference points)
- Camera / phone to photograph belt routing before removal

## Tips

- Take multiple photos of the belt routing and all timing marks before removal — you will thank yourself during installation.
- Mark the belt rotation direction with an arrow before removal — install the new belt in the same direction.
- The crankshaft pulley bolt is extremely tight. An impact wrench is the safest way to remove it — trying to break it loose with a breaker bar risks breaking the crankshaft position sensor.
- Check the condition of the cam seals and front crankshaft seal while the covers are off — replace them if weeping. Seal kits are cheap; re-doing the timing belt to fix a seal leak is expensive.
- After completing the job, mark the date and odometer on a sticker inside the engine bay.
