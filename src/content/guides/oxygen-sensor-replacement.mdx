The 6G75 V6 petrol engine uses four oxygen sensors (two upstream, two downstream — one per bank) to manage fuel trim and monitor catalytic converter efficiency. A faulty O2 sensor causes rich or lean running, increased fuel consumption, and catalyst damage if left unchecked. Fault codes P0130–P0167 (sensor circuit) and P0420/P0430 (catalyst efficiency) are the most common triggers. Sensors typically last 100,000–150,000 km.

## Symptoms

- Check Engine light with P0130–P0167 (O2 sensor circuit fault)
- P0420 or P0430 — catalyst efficiency below threshold (downstream sensor)
- Increased fuel consumption with no other obvious cause
- Rough idle or hunting idle (rich/lean signal causing fuel trim correction)
- Fuel smell from exhaust (overly rich mixture from failed upstream sensor)

## Tools needed

- O2 sensor socket (22mm with wire slot)
- Breaker bar (sensors often seized)
- Anti-seize compound (for installation)
- OBD-II scanner (to clear codes)

## Tips

- Soak sensor threads with penetrating oil (WD-40 or similar) and let it dwell for 30 minutes before removal — sensors seize in the bung after years of heat cycling.
- Replace sensors with the engine cold, not hot — hot metal expands and can strip the thread if the sensor is seized.
- Apply anti-seize to the new sensor threads only — keep it off the last 10 mm near the sensing tip.
- On the 6G75, the upstream sensors are accessible from above (Bank 2, radiator side) and from under the car (Bank 1, firewall side).
- If P0420 appears after replacing only the upstream sensor, the catalyst itself may be damaged. Check downstream sensor voltage before condemning the catalyst.
- After replacement, clear the fault code and drive one full warm-up cycle. The ECU needs to complete its readiness monitors before reporting pass/fail.
