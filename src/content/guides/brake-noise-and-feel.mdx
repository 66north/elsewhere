Brake noise (squealing, grinding, creaking) and poor feel (soft pedal, extended stopping distance) are often signs of worn pads, moisture in fluid, or air in the brake lines. While some noise is cosmetic, grinding and a soft pedal indicate safety issues. Diagnosis is straightforward: inspect pads for wear, check fluid condition, and bleed air if the pedal is soft.

## Symptoms

- High-pitched squealing when braking (usually front pads, often ceramic pads are louder)
- Grinding sound and metal-on-metal feel (brake pads worn to backing plate)
- Soft or spongy brake pedal (air in lines or moisture in fluid)
- Extended stopping distance (degraded pads, moisture in fluid, or air in system)
- Brake pedal fades during repeated hard braking (fluid boiling due to high temperature)
- Uneven braking (one wheel locks up before others, or vehicle pulls to one side)
- Creaking noise when braking (suspension or brake caliper movement, not pad wear)

## Tools needed

- Jack and jack stands (to safely lift vehicle)
- Wheel chocks (to prevent rolling)
- Socket set for caliper bolts
- Brake fluid (DOT 3 or DOT 4)
- Bleed kit or manual pump (if bleeding brakes)

## Tips

- Brake noise is usually not a safety issue unless it's grinding (metal-on-metal). Squealing is annoying but your brakes still work
- Brake pads are a wear item; expect to replace them every 40,000-80,000 km depending on driving style (frequent hard braking wears pads faster)
- Brake rotors often last 100,000+ km if pads are changed before metal touches rotor. Let pads wear too long and rotors will need replacement too
- Moisture in brake fluid lowers boiling point from 205°C (dry) to 140°C (saturated). This is why fluid flush is recommended every 2 years
- If your brakes feel different after a repair, it may just be new pads bedding in (first 100 km). Pedal feel will normalize
