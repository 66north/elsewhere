The Pajero IV uses disc brakes on all four wheels. Rear pads typically last 60,000–80,000 km depending on driving style. Rear caliper pistons rotate as they compress (unlike front pistons which press straight in), so you need a brake piston wind-back tool — a screwdriver will not work and will damage the caliper. Always replace rear pads as a complete axle set (both sides).

## Symptoms

- Squealing or grinding noise from rear brakes
- Brake pad warning light illuminated (if rear sensors fitted)
- Rear pad thickness below 3mm when inspected
- Vehicle pulling to one side under braking
- Pulsing or vibration through brake pedal

## Tools needed

- 17mm socket (wheel bolts)
- 12mm + 14mm socket (caliper bolts)
- Brake piston wind-back tool (cube/socket type for rotating pistons — essential)
- Torque wrench
- Wire brush (caliper slides)

## Tips

- The rear caliper pistons ROTATE as they compress — you cannot push them straight in with a clamp or screwdriver. A wind-back tool is mandatory.
- Wind-back direction: clockwise when viewed from behind the piston (check your specific caliper — direction markings are usually moulded in).
- Clean the caliper slide pins with a wire brush and apply fresh brake lubricant. Seized slides are the most common cause of uneven rear pad wear.
- Before compressing pistons, remove about 50ml of fluid from the reservoir — the pistons displacing back will overflow the reservoir if it's full.
- Bed in new pads with 10–15 moderate stops from 60 km/h before any hard braking. This seats the pad compound to the disc surface.
