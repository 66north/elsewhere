Engine overheating is a serious condition that can cause head gasket failure, warped cylinder heads, or catastrophic engine damage if ignored. Most overheating is caused by coolant loss (leak or evaporation), failed thermostat, or weak water pump. Diagnosis requires understanding the cooling system components and ruling out causes methodically. Never ignore an overheating warning light or high temperature gauge.

## Symptoms

- Temperature gauge reads above the midpoint or near red zone
- Steam or smoke from under the hood (coolant boiling in radiator)
- Sweet smell from the engine bay (coolant leak or burning)
- Coolant leaking from radiator hose, radiator, or engine block
- Check Engine light illuminated (P0128 - thermostat fault code)
- Loss of heater output (low coolant or airlock in system)
- Engine knocking or pinging under load (pre-ignition due to heat)

## Tools needed

- Coolant thermometer (optional, to measure actual temperatures)
- Compressed air or soft brush to clean radiator fins
- Wrench set for hose clamp removal
- Gasket scraper (if replacing thermostat)
- Funnel and container for coolant

## Tips

- Modern Pajeros have a thermostat that is designed to fail closed (safe) rather than open. If stuck open, the engine will run cold, not hot
- Check the radiator cap — a bad cap cannot hold pressure, causing coolant to boil at a lower temperature. Caps are inexpensive (5-10 dollars) and often overlooked
- If overheating occurs only when towing or in traffic (not on the highway), suspect fan failure or low coolant. Highway driving keeps the engine cooler due to airflow
- Mixing old and new coolant can cause precipitation and blockage. Always flush and refill with fresh coolant when doing major repairs
- If the engine overheats despite all repairs, suspect internal engine damage (cracked head, blown gasket). Have a professional inspect with a pressure test
