The Pajero IV automatic transmission has an oil cooler integrated into the radiator (a heat exchanger in the lower tank) plus an external cooler on some models. The cooler passages become contaminated with burnt fluid and debris over time. A cooler flush is recommended when replacing the transmission fluid after the transmission has overheated, or when fluid is very dark and smells burnt. It is done in addition to the transmission fluid change, not instead of it.

## Symptoms

- Transmission fluid very dark or smells burnt (overheating history)
- Transmission fluid changed but shifting quality did not improve
- Transmission overheating warning or harsh shifting in hot weather
- Dark sludge visible when draining the transmission pan

## Tools needed

- Drain pan (minimum 10L capacity)
- 10mm socket (transmission pan bolts)
- Transmission cooler line disconnect tool or flat blade
- Hand fluid pump
- Torque wrench

## Tips

- The most practical flush method on a home driveway: disconnect the cooler return line (the line from the cooler back to the transmission), place it in a container, and run the engine for 5–10 seconds at a time, topping up the transmission fluid between short runs. Repeat until clean fluid exits.
- Do not run the engine for more than 10–15 seconds without adding fluid — running the transmission dry damages the pump.
- Identify the cooler return line: it is the lower of the two lines connecting the transmission to the radiator cooler. On the Pajero IV it connects at the transmission side with a push-fit or banjo fitting.
- If the existing fluid is black with a burnt smell, consider dropping the transmission pan first to clean the magnet and inspect for debris before flushing the cooler.
- Cheap ATF degrades faster in a cooler — always use SP-III. If using an aftermarket fluid, confirm it meets Mitsubishi SP-III specification.
