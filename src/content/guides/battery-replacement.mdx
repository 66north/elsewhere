The 12V automotive battery powers the starter, alternator, and all electrical systems. Most batteries last 4–6 years before losing capacity, slower cold-start performance, and eventual complete failure. Replacement is one of the simplest maintenance tasks, requiring only basic hand tools and 15 minutes. Modern batteries use lead-acid chemistry and should be recycled — many retailers offer core return credits.

## Symptoms

- Engine cranks slowly (sluggish, weak starter turn)
- Difficulty starting in cold weather (below 5°C)
- Dimming headlights during engine cranking or when running electrical accessories
- Click-click-click when turning the key (starter solenoid cannot pull in due to low voltage)
- Dashboard warning light for battery/alternator (yellow battery icon)
- Battery case is visibly swollen or cracked (internal short or overcharge condition)

## Tools needed

- Open-end or adjustable wrench (10mm or 13mm, depending on terminal bolt size)
- Screwdrivers (flathead and Phillips, for battery hold-down and corrosion removal)
- Wire brush or steel wool (to clean corroded battery terminals)
- Battery terminal puller (optional, if terminals are stuck fast)
- Baking soda and water (to neutralize spilled battery acid)
- Gloves and eye protection (lead-acid batteries contain hazardous material)

## Tips

- Turn off all electrical accessories and the ignition before starting. This reduces the risk of short-circuiting the alternator
- The negative (black) battery terminal should be disconnected FIRST, then the positive (red) terminal. This sequence prevents accidental shorts
- If the battery terminals are heavily corroded (white or blue-green crusty buildup), disconnect and clean them with a wire brush and baking soda slurry before installation
- When installing the new battery, connect the POSITIVE (red) terminal first, then the NEGATIVE (black). This is the reverse of disconnection and minimizes spark risk
- Ensure the battery is sitting flat on the hold-down tray and the bracket is tightened. A loose battery can tip and damage the case or cause intermittent electrical issues
- After installation, start the engine and verify the alternator is charging (voltmeter should read 13.5–14.5V at idle, 14.0–14.8V at 2000 RPM)
- If the engine does not start after battery replacement, or if the new battery discharges overnight, the alternator may be faulty. Test the alternator output before replacing the battery again
- Recycle your old battery at an auto parts store or recycling center. Most retailers offer a $5–$10 core credit, offsetting part of the cost
- Battery terminals can be protected from future corrosion with felt washers or dielectric grease applied to the terminal posts
