Wheel alignment sets the angles of the tyres relative to the vehicle and road. Correct alignment prevents uneven tyre wear, reduces rolling resistance, and keeps the vehicle tracking straight. The Pajero IV uses an independent front suspension and a live rear axle, so front alignment (toe and camber) is adjustable; rear alignment is largely fixed. Alignment should be checked after any suspension component replacement, kerb strike, or if the vehicle pulls to one side.

## Symptoms

- Vehicle pulling left or right when driving straight on a level road
- Uneven tyre wear — worn on inner or outer edge only
- Steering wheel off-centre when driving straight
- Vibration or wandering at highway speeds
- After suspension component replacement (arms, ball joints, tie rods)
- After a heavy kerb strike or pothole impact

## Tips

- Always check tyre pressures before an alignment — under-inflated tyres change the contact patch and make alignment readings inaccurate.
- Front toe is the most commonly out-of-spec measurement and causes rapid inner or outer tyre wear. Easy to see on worn tyres before alignment is measured.
- Camber on the Pajero IV front suspension is partially adjustable via eccentric bolts on the lower arm. Some workshops skip this and only adjust toe.
- After any steering or suspension repair (tie rod, lower arm, ball joint), always get an alignment — the geometry will have changed.
- Alignment costs $60–120 at most tyre shops. It's the cheapest tyre preservation service you can buy.
- If the vehicle pulls only under braking (not at cruise), the issue is brake balance or a sticking caliper, not alignment.
- Off-road driving on rough terrain gradually knocks alignment out. Check annually if you drive off-road regularly.
