Diesel engines require glow plugs to preheat the combustion chamber for cold starts. The 4M41 has four glow plugs, one per cylinder, controlled by the engine ECU. A failed glow plug causes hard cold starts, white smoke on startup, and rough idling until the engine warms. If all four glow plugs fail, the engine becomes nearly impossible to start below 5°C. Replacement is straightforward but requires care to avoid breaking the ceramic element inside the bore.

## Symptoms

- Difficulty starting in cold weather (below 10°C)
- White smoke on cold startup (incomplete combustion)
- Rough idle for 30+ seconds after a cold start
- Check Engine light with glow plug-related code (P0380, P0671–P0674)
- Hesitation or stumbling during warm-up phase
- One or more glow plug indicator lights not illuminating on the dashboard

## Tools needed

- Glow plug socket (usually 10mm or 12mm hex bit, with a rubber insert to protect ceramic element)
- Standard socket wrench and ratchet
- Spark plug gap tool or feeler gauge (to check plug burn condition if inspecting old plugs)
- Thermal jacket or insulated socket (recommended to avoid shocking the ceramic with temperature changes)
- Engine cover removal tools (plastic clips or screws, depending on year/model)

## Tips

- Glow plugs are located on top of the engine, under a plastic cover (labeled "ENGINE" or similar). Carefully remove plastic clips to expose them.
- The glow plug connectors are a single multi-pin connector block on the driver's side. You may need to unclip it to access the plugs, or work around it.
- If a plug breaks inside the bore during removal, stop and consult a professional. Broken ceramic can score the cylinder head and require machine work.
- Cold soak the vehicle overnight before replacement if possible — cold metal breaks less easily than warm. Do not remove glow plugs from a hot engine.
- After installing new plugs, allow the engine to idle for 2–3 minutes before driving. The ECU learns glow plug resistance on startup.
- Premature glow plug failure (before 80,000 km) often indicates a fuel injection timing issue or water in the fuel. If you replace plugs and they fail again quickly, have the injection pump checked.
- Some 2008–2009 models use MD319913 instead of MD319950. Always cross-reference your VIN with a dealer parts catalog.
