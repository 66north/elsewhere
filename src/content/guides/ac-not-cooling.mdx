The Pajero IV uses a dual-zone automatic climate control system with front and rear outlets. When the AC stops cooling, the cause is usually low refrigerant (slow leak), a failed compressor clutch, a blocked condenser, or a faulty pressure sensor. This guide walks through diagnosing the most common causes before committing to a regas or compressor replacement.

## Symptoms

- Air from vents is warm or only slightly cool
- Cooling works only when driving at speed (condenser airflow-dependent fault)
- AC compressor not cycling on (clutch not engaging)
- AC light illuminated but no cold air
- System blows cold briefly then reverts to warm
- Rear AC not working but front is fine (separate blower motor fault)

## Tips

- Check the cabin air filter first — a blocked filter severely restricts airflow and makes the AC appear to not cool even when the refrigerant charge is correct.
- With engine running and AC on, listen for the compressor clutch engaging — a click from the front of the engine bay followed by a slight RPM drop confirms the compressor is running.
- No click = compressor not engaging. Could be low refrigerant (safety cutout), failed clutch, or blown AC fuse.
- Check the AC fuse in the engine bay fuse box before any other diagnosis — a blown fuse is a 2-minute fix.
- Cooling that works at highway speed but fails in traffic usually means the condenser fan is not running — check the condenser fan fuse and relay.
- If the system cools briefly then stops, the high-pressure cutout is tripping — suspect overcharged refrigerant or a blocked condenser.
- Refrigerant leaks are the most common cause of gradual AC failure on a 10+ year-old vehicle. A UV dye test or electronic sniffer confirms the leak location.
- AC recharges (regassing) are a workshop job requiring certified refrigerant handling equipment — do not attempt to top up with DIY cans on a system that hasn't been leak-tested.
