The V6 engines (6G72 3.0L and 6G74 3.5L) use hydraulic lash adjusters (HLA) instead of manually-adjusted valve clearances. These HLAs are filled with oil under pressure to automatically maintain zero valve clearance. After sitting overnight, oil drains from the adjuster and causes a ticking noise on start-up — this is normal and should clear within 30–60 seconds. Persistent or loud ticking after warm-up indicates air in the adjuster, incorrect oil level, or worn/dirty HLA units.

## Symptoms

- Ticking or clattering from the valve train on cold start, clearing within 30–60 seconds (normal)
- Persistent ticking after the engine is fully warmed up (abnormal)
- Ticking that remains after repeated throttle cycling exercises
- Loud clatter at idle that does not improve

## Tips

- Fresh engine oil is essential — sludged oil cannot flow quickly enough to keep HLAs pressurised.
- If the vehicle has been sitting for months, oil has fully drained from the HLAs and ticking may take several minutes to clear on first start — this is normal.
- Use the correct viscosity oil (typically 10W-30 or 5W-30 per Mitsubishi spec). Thick oil at cold temperatures is slow to prime the HLAs.
- If one HLA is worn or stuck, the tick will be localised to that cylinder. A mechanic with a stethoscope can pinpoint the noisy cylinder.
- Persistent HLA ticking despite fresh oil and air-bleeding usually means the adjuster is worn and needs replacement — this is an engine-out job.
- If the oil looks milky (coolant contamination) or is very dark and smells burnt, change the oil before attempting the air-bleed procedure.
