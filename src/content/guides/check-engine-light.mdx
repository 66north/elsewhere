The Check Engine Light (CEL), also called the Malfunction Indicator Lamp (MIL), illuminates whenever the engine ECU detects an emission or engine performance fault. The light alone does not tell you what the problem is — you need a diagnostic scanner to read the fault code. Some faults are minor (faulty oxygen sensor) and car remains drivable; others are serious (timing retarded, misfire) and should be addressed immediately. This guide explains the different types of CEL conditions and how to diagnose them.

## Symptoms

- Check Engine Light illuminated on the instrument cluster (either steady or blinking)
- Blinking/flashing light = active misfire (severe condition, stop driving immediately)
- Steady light = fault logged, vehicle is drivable (inspect for secondary symptoms)
- Reduced engine power or limp-mode (ECU cuts cylinders or limits boost to protect engine)
- Rough idle, hesitation, or poor acceleration
- Increased fuel consumption or black smoke
- No apparent symptoms (many electrical faults trigger CEL without noticeable change)

## Tools needed

- OBD2 diagnostic scanner (loaned free at auto parts stores or purchased for 30-60 dollars)
- Flashlight and screwdrivers to inspect engine for loose wires and corrosion

## Tips

- Do NOT clear the Check Engine Light without understanding the fault. Many shops clear codes and take your money without fixing anything. The light comes back shortly after
- Some codes are intermittent. You may see a code like P0171 (lean) logged, but the condition clears itself if you drive on the highway. This is often a loose connector or a sensor entering/exiting a failure window
- [object Object]
- Avoid disconnecting the battery to clear the CEL. This erases all diagnostic history and makes troubleshooting harder. Use a scanner to clear codes after fixing the fault
- [object Object]
- Professional diagnosis costs $100–$200 at an independent shop but saves money vs. guessing and replacing parts randomly
