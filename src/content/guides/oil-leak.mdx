Oil leaks are one of the most common complaints on older Pajero models. While some leaks are minor (slow seepage), others indicate serious problems (blown gaskets, cracked block). The key is identifying the leak source quickly before oil starvation causes engine damage. Most leaks are from worn gaskets or seals and are manageable repairs; a few require professional intervention.

## Symptoms

- Oil spots or puddles under the vehicle (check color and location)
- Burning oil smell from the engine bay or exhaust
- Low oil level on dipstick despite recent top-up
- Visible oil residue on engine block, valve cover, or pan
- Black or brown discoloration on engine exterior (accumulated oil)
- Smoke from the engine during acceleration (oil dripping on hot surfaces)

## Tools needed

- Gasket scraper or plastic scraper to remove old gasket material
- Socket set and screwdrivers for bolt removal
- Torque wrench for reinstallation
- Flashlight to inspect source
- Jack and jack stands (if accessing pan or underside)
- Clean rags and shop towels

## Tips

- Do not ignore small leaks. A slow 5 mL/day leak becomes 150 mL/month and can starve the engine of oil
- Always replace gaskets, never reuse. Old gaskets are compressed and lose sealing ability
- When removing a valve cover or pan, support the engine with a block of wood or engine hoist to avoid straining adjacent components
- Apply a thin bead of gasket maker (RTV silicone) on both sides of the new gasket for a better seal
- Tighten gasket bolts in a cross pattern (alternating opposite sides) to ensure even seating
- After repair, monitor oil level for 500 km to confirm the leak has stopped
- If a leak returns within weeks of repair, the gasket may not have seated properly or bolts may have loosened; re-torque and inspect
