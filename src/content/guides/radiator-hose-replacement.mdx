The Pajero IV has two main radiator hoses — upper and lower — plus a smaller overflow/header tank hose. Rubber hoses degrade and eventually crack or split, causing catastrophic coolant loss. They should be inspected at every service (squeeze the hose when cold — it should feel firm and spring back, not mushy or hard and cracked). A 10-year or 150,000 km replacement interval is conservative but sensible for a vehicle used off-road.

## Symptoms

- Coolant leak visible at hose or hose clamp connection
- Hose feels soft and mushy when squeezed cold (internal delamination)
- Hose is hard and cracked when squeezed (heat-hardened, failure imminent)
- Engine temperature rising faster than normal (restricted hose reducing flow)
- Coolant smell from engine bay without visible leak (seeping past clamp)

## Tools needed

- Pliers or hose clamp tool (spring clamps)
- Drain pan (minimum 3L)
- Hose pick or blunt knife (to break hose seal)

## Tips

- Always replace hoses with the engine cold — never open the cooling system when hot.
- To break the seal of a stuck hose: twist it before pulling, or carefully use a hose pick around the rim. Do not pry with a screwdriver — the plastic thermostat housing and radiator neck are fragile.
- Check the hose clamps when replacing hoses — original spring-type clamps weaken over time. Replace with screw-type clamps for better long-term retention.
- Burp the coolant system after refilling: leave the heater on full, start the engine, and wait until the top hose gets hot (thermostat opens). Then check the level again and top up if needed.
- Inspect the header/overflow tank hoses at the same time — they are small in diameter and often overlooked.
- If replacing only one hose, check the age and condition of the other before reassembly. Labour is the same either way.
