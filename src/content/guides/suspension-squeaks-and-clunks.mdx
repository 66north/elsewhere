Suspension noises — squeaks, creaks, or clunks — typically indicate worn bushings, loose bolts, or failed dampers (shocks). Unlike engine knocks, suspension noises are often intermittent and depend on driving conditions (rough road, turning, braking). Most suspension noises are not safety-critical but warrant investigation to prevent secondary damage (e.g., a loose sway bar link can wear a hole in the steering knuckle if left unaddressed). This guide walks you through identifying the source and determining repair urgency.

## Tips

- Many DIY mechanics mistake suspension noises for engine knocks. If the noise is clearly from the wheel wells and not under the hood, it is suspension
- Suspension squeaks and creaks are worst in cold weather (morning starts) when rubber is stiff and brittle. Noise often improves as the vehicle warms up
- [object Object]
- If you are handy, sway bar bushing and link replacement are good DIY projects. Ball joints and control arm bushing removal usually require a press or special tools, warranting professional service
- Have a friend listen while driving to localize the noise. Front-end suspension changes affect the entire vehicle handling, so the noise location is critical
- Vibration and noise can also come from the exhaust, engine mounts, or body rattles. Isolate the noise by turning off the radio, closing windows, and listening carefully
