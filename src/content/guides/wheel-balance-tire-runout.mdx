Wheel imbalance (uneven weight distribution) and tire runout (out-of-round or side-to-side wobble) cause vibration and accelerated tire wear. Factory service intervals call for wheel balance checks after tire replacement or if vibration develops. Runout is a tire or wheel defect measured with a dial gauge; balance is corrected by adding weights. DIY diagnosis is visual and tactile; precision measurement requires a wheel balance machine at a tyre shop.

## Symptoms

- Vibration in the steering wheel or floor at highway speeds (70–100 km/h)
- Vibration that decreases at higher speeds or appears only within a narrow speed range
- Uneven tire wear (one edge wearing faster than the opposite edge)
- Tyre wear on one side of the tread pattern (cupping or scalloping)
- Visible wobble or run-out when spinning the wheel by hand (out-of-round)
- Steering wheel shimmy on acceleration or braking

## Tools needed

- Dial indicator or runout gauge (tyre shop equipment)
- Wheel balancer machine (tyre shop equipment)
- Jack and jack stands

## Tips

- Imbalance vs runout: imbalance causes up-and-down vibration, runout causes wobble. Imbalance is corrected by adding weights; runout is corrected by truing the tire or replacing the wheel.
- Visual check for runout: spin the wheel by hand (with the vehicle jacked up) and watch the edge of the tire. It should remain perfectly round with no side-to-side wobble. Any visible wobble indicates runout.
- Vibration at one specific speed (e.g., exactly at 80 km/h) is often imbalance — the resonant frequency of the car matches a harmonic of the wheel rotation. Vibration across a range of speeds is more likely runout or a bearing problem.
- After tire replacement, always have wheels balanced. Even a new tire can have manufacturing imbalance. Balancing adds $15–25 per wheel to a tire job.
- Stick-on weights (adhesive) are lighter and look neater on alloy wheels. Clip-on weights work on steel wheels and are reusable. The choice depends on your wheel type.
- Runout tolerance: typically ±3 mm radial (vertical wobble) and ±2 mm lateral (side-to-side). Wheels exceeding this should be re-trued or replaced.
- If vibration persists after balancing, suspect driveshaft runout, engine imbalance, or suspension wear — not a wheel problem.
